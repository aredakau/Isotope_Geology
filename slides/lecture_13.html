Principles of Mass Spectrometry

Source: https://microbenotes.com

  • Definition: Mass spectrometry is an analytical technique used to measure the mass-to-charge ratio of ions.
  • Importance: commonly use for analysis of isotopes and geochemical data.

Basic Principles of Operation

  1. Ionization: Converts atoms/molecules into ions.
  2. Acceleration: Ion beam is accelerated to uniform kinetic energy.
  3. Mass-to-Charge Separation: Analyzer separates ions based on their mass-to-charge ratio.
  4. Detection: Measures the abundance of separated ions to generate a mass spectrum.
  5. High Vacuum Conditions: Reduces ion collisions, ensuring accurate measurements.

Components of Mass Spectrometers

Source: WikiDoc

  1. Ionizer (Ion Source): Generates ions from the sample.
  2. Mass Analyzer: Separates ions by mass-to-charge ratio.
  3. Ion Detector: Captures and quantifies ions.
  4. Vacuum System: Maintains high vacuum for accurate ion movement.
  5. Data System: Processes and analyzes mass spectra.

Types of Mass Analyzers

  1. Quadrupole Analyzer: Uses oscillating electric fields to filter ions.
  2. Time-of-Flight (TOF): Measures time for ions to travel a fixed distance.
  3. Ion Trap Analyzer: Captures and manipulates ions in a confined space.
  4. Magnetic Sector Analyzer: Deflects ions using a magnetic field based on mass.
  5. FT-ICR: Ultra-high resolution using ion cyclotron resonance.

Sample Introduction Methods

  • Gas: Coupled with gas chromatography.
  • Liquid: Coupled with liquid chromatography.
  • Solid: Direct injection (e.g., laser ablation).

Ionization Techniques

  1. Electron Ionization (EI): Example: Magnetic sector instruments.
  2. Chemical Ionization (CI): Example: Quadrupole instruments.
  3. Electrospray Ionization (ESI): Example: TOF instruments.
  4. Matrix-Assisted Laser Desorption/Ionization (MALDI): Example: MALDI-TOF instruments.

Ion Detection Systems

  1. Electron Multipliers: Amplifies ion signals.
  2. Faraday Cups: Measures ion current directly.
  3. Array Detectors: Simultaneous detection of multiple ions.

Mass Spectra Interpretation

  1. Mass-to-Charge Ratio (m/z): Identifies ions.
  2. Isotope Patterns: Reveals isotope composition.
  3. Fragmentation Patterns: Analyzes molecular structure.
  4. Molecular Ion Peaks: Indicates molecular weight.

Quantitative Analysis

  1. Calibration Methods: Relates signal intensity to ion concentration.
  2. Internal Standards: Enhances measurement precision.
  3. Isotope Dilution: Ensures accurate quantification.

Limitations and Challenges

  1. Matrix Effects: Interference from sample composition.
  2. Isobaric Interferences: Overlapping ions with similar m/z.
  3. Instrument Sensitivity: Detecting low-abundance ions.
