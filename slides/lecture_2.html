Atomic Structure

  • Atom: The basic unit of matter, consisting of a nucleus surrounded by electrons.
  • Nucleus: The atomâs core, containing protons and neutrons.
  • Electrons: Negatively charged particles orbiting the nucleus in electron shells.

Key Components

  • Protons:
    • Positively charged particles.
    • Located in the nucleus.
    • Determine the atomic number and identity of the element.
  • Neutrons:
    • Neutral particles (no charge).
    • Also located in the nucleus.
    • Contribute to the atomic mass and stability of the atom.
  • Electrons:
    • Negatively charged particles.
    • Orbit the nucleus in defined energy levels (shells).
    • Involved in chemical bonding and reactions.

Nuclear Definitions & Mass Units

  • N: Number of neutrons
  • Z: Number of protons (Atomic number)
  • A: Mass number (A = N + Z)
  • M: Atomic mass
  • I: Neutron excess (I = N - Z)

Isotopes, Isobars, and Isotones

  • Isotopes: Same number of protons, different numbers of neutrons.
  • Example: Carbon isotopes
    • \(^ {12}\text{C}\): 6 protons, 6 neutrons
    • \(^ {13}\text{C}\): 6 protons, 7 neutrons
    • \(^ {14}\text{C}\): 6 protons, 8 neutrons
  • Isobars: Same mass number (A).
  • Example:
    • Rubidium-Strontium (Rb-Sr):
      • \(^ {87}\text{Rb}\): 37 protons, 50 neutrons (Mass number = 87)
      • \(^ {87}\text{Sr}\): 38 protons, 49 neutrons (Mass number = 87)
    • Lutetium-Hafnium (Lu-Hf):
      • \(^ {176}\text{Lu}\): 71 protons, 105 neutrons (Mass number = 176)
      • \(^ {176}\text{Hf}\): 72 protons, 104 neutrons (Mass number = 176)
  • Isotones: Same number of neutrons, different number of protons.
  • Example: Isotones of Neutrons
    • \(^ {14}\text{N}\): 7 protons, 7 neutrons
    • \(^ {15}\text{O}\): 8 protons, 7 neutrons
    • \(^ {16}\text{F}\): 9 protons, 7 neutrons

Atomic Mass & Atomic Number

  • Atomic Number (Z): Number of protons in the nucleus, defining the element.
  • Mass Number (A): Sum of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.

Unit of nuclear mass

  • The basic unit of nuclear mass is the unified atomic mass unit u (also known as the dalton and the atomic mass unit or amu)

Masses of Atomic Particles
- Proton: 1.0073 u
- Neutron: 1.0087 u
- Electron: 0.00055 u

Why is the Atomic Mass Often Not a Whole Number?

For example, if we look at the mass of \(^4\text{He}\), it can be calculated as:

\[ 1.0073 + 1.0073 + 1.0087 + 1.0087 = 4.032 \]

However, this is not accurate, as the actual mass is 4.0026 (see source).

Why is the actual mass less than the expected mass, which is the sum of the particle masses (2 protons and 2 neutrons)? see next slide.

Mass Defect and Binding Energy

  • Mass Defect:
    • The nucleus has a lower mass than the sum of its individual protons and neutrons.
    • This difference is known as the mass defect.
  • Binding Energy:
    • The mass defect results from the energy required to bind nucleons together.
    • This energy, called binding energy, is released when the nucleus forms.

Example: Helium-4 (\(^4\text{He}\))

  • Sum of Individual Masses: Greater than the mass of the \(^4\text{He}\) nucleus.
  • Binding Energy: The mass difference is due to the binding energy that holds the nucleus together.

So The actual mass of \(^4\text{He}\) is less than the expected mass due to the conversion of mass into binding energy, which keeps the nucleons together within the nucleus.

Isotopes in Geosciences

  • Radioactive Isotopes:
    • Definition: Isotopes that are unstable and decay over time, emitting radiation.
    • Uses: Dating geological materials, and constrain timing of geological processes.
    • Examples: Carbon-14, Uranium-238, Rubidium-87.
  • Stable Isotopes:
    • Definition: Isotopes that do not decay over time and remain stable.
    • Uses: Tracing environmental and climatic changes, studying rock and mineral formation.
    • Examples: Oxygen-18, Lead-206, Strontium-86.

Applications in Geosciences

  • Radiometric Dating:
    • Uranium-Lead Dating: Used to date rocks and minerals, providing age estimates for the Earthâs formation.
    • Potassium-Argon Dating: Useful for dating volcanic rocks and understanding volcanic activity.
  • Climate and Environmental Reconstruction:
    • Oxygen Isotope Ratios: Used to reconstruct past temperatures and climate conditions from ice cores and marine sediments.
    • Carbon Isotope Ratios: Helps in understanding past vegetation types and climate conditions.
  • Tracing Geological Processes:
    • Strontium Isotopes: Used to trace the sources of sediment and understand tectonic and erosion processes.
    • Lead Isotopes: Useful in studying the origin and movement of mineral deposits and understanding ore genesis.
  • Paleontology and Fossil Studies:
    • Carbon-14 Dating: Employed for dating relatively young fossils and archaeological artifacts.
